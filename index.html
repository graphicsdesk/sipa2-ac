On March 13, 2025, the federal government demanded that Columbia place its Middle Eastern, South Asian, and African studies department under academic receivership.

The demand, which would have stripped the department’s governance away from its faculty members and installed an external chair, was one of nine requirements for the University to begin talks to restore federal funding the White House canceled days earlier. But it was immediately recognized as the most invasive. Though universities, including Columbia, had placed departments under receivership in the past, they had done so only of their own volition, as a last resort to address severe dysfunction.

Columbia never agreed to the unprecedented federal demand.

Eight days later, even with billions of dollars of research funding at stake, the University pledged only to conduct a “thorough review” of its regional studies portfolio to ensure that its offerings were “comprehensive and balanced,” beginning “immediately” with programs focused on the Middle East.

Columbia posted the initial recommendations from the review, conducted by the provost’s office, in February. Rather than recommending changes to MESAAS, the review committee focused on expanding programs in the social sciences that would be “clearly differentiated from” MESAAS and its traditional focus on the humanities, including a new undergraduate major or minor focused on the Middle East.

Though the review committee deferred setting its newly proposed Middle East program’s “final designation” to a later date, it suggested that its “logical initial — and potentially long-term — location” would be as a track within a new undergraduate major offered by the School of International and Public Affairs.

The SIPA major, which Columbia had rapidly advanced through the approval process for the past several months, had been at the heart of a fierce debate among faculty over the future of a liberal arts education, given that SIPA is a professional school. Now, the University has thrust it to the center of an even more incendiary dispute over the future of scholarship about the Middle East.

The major will launch in the fall, and some of its courses have already begun enrolling students.

Columbia to expand Middle East coursework with fall rollout of SIPA undergraduate major, confidential proposals show

By Matt Luo and Celine Chien

July 6, 2026

Columbia has approved plans for SIPA to pilot an undergraduate major in global affairs and public policy for the next three years, according to a May memo obtained by Spectator. The program, Columbia’s first to be primarily taught through a graduate professional school outside the Faculty of Arts and Sciences, will cap its enrollment at 100 students next year, selected through a lottery.

In documents obtained by Spectator, SIPA proposed several regional studies offerings for the major. However, it anticipated implementing courses on the Middle East, most of which focus on Israel, before those on any other region.

“We knew we were going to do Middle East, and part of the reason why we were going to do Middle East is because of the provost’s report. So, we knew there was an appetite for that, and we knew that it would be supported,” Lisa Anderson, GSAS ’81, former dean of SIPA said. Anderson is a member of both the provost’s committee that recommended a new Middle East program and the faculty committee developing the SIPA major.

In light of the major’s focus on the Middle East, some students and faculty members saw the SIPA major as a way to offer an alternative to MESAAS without touching the politically explosive issue of its independence.

In its July 2025 settlement with the federal government, which restored funding and resolved civil rights investigations into alleged antisemitic harassment at Columbia, the University legally bound itself to review regional studies and appoint several new faculty members jointly between the Institute for Israel and Jewish Studies and either SIPA, the political science department, or the economics department. The appointments represented a move to ensure a “robust and intellectually diverse academic environment.”

“This was a very difficult time for the University,” Stuart Firestein, a faculty senator and member of the committee which approved the SIPA major, said. “Anything we could do to get the government mollified, off our backs, within reason, didn’t seem like, I assume, an awful thing to do.” He claimed, though, that the deal never came up in his discussions about the SIPA major.

Former acting University President Claire Shipman, CC ’86, SIPA ’94, denied that the SIPA major was connected to Columbia’s negotiations with President Donald Trump’s administration. In a June 18 statement to the University Senate, she wrote that the major “reflects longstanding undergraduate interest in this area of study” and was “a Columbia academic initiative and ours alone.”

The program could allow SIPA, whose student body is 63 percent international, to capitalize on a largely domestic undergraduate student body and attract donors. Following the Trump administration’s crackdown on international students, Columbia experienced a Universitywide decline in international student applications in fall 2025.

As part of its deal with the government, Columbia also agreed to reduce its “financial dependence on international student enrollment.”

A University official denied that Columbia took into account enrollment trends in creating the major or that it developed the program in response to its review of regional studies. Instead, they said the impetus for the major was the state of the world and the complexity of its policy challenges.

The idea of expanding regional studies at Columbia, especially focused on the Middle East and Israel, had existed long before Trump and the regional studies review, as did accusations that MESAAS faculty were antisemitic.

SIPA had considered offering an undergraduate major for years, according to several of its faculty members. The school finally brought a proposal in late September 2025 to the committee on instruction, the faculty-led body which approves all academic programs for Columbia College and the School of General Studies.

The COI provisionally approved the major just three months later, and it issued its final approval on May 12, during its final meeting of the academic year, according to a person familiar with the committee’s deliberations who was not authorized to speak publicly on the confidential proceedings.

The central administration prioritized advancing the major “as expeditiously as possible,” according to a December 2025 memo from the COI obtained by Spectator, sparking sharp criticism among faculty members who said that the University pressured them to rush it through the approval process.

In its May memo outlining the stipulations for its approval, obtained by Spectator, the COI wrote that it would “work closely” with SIPA to monitor enrollment in the major and its impact on FAS departments, basing any decision to increase the 100-student cap on holistic annual assessments.

SIPA had proposed a phased approach, enrolling 100, 125, and then 150 students over the next three years.

All individual courses for the major must still secure COI approval separately, and the COI stipulated that total enrollment in the major not exceed 275 students over the next three years, after which the committee would conduct a “full review.”

Two of the new introductory courses for the major have already begun enrolling students on Vergil for the fall, after the COI approved them April 10.

The University has yet to release more information about the program as of publication, though a University official told Spectator that Columbia will officially announce the major later this month.

Spectator conducted interviews with 14 students and faculty members spanning both SIPA and political science faculty as well as members of the committees responsible for reviewing the program, including 10 people with direct knowledge of SIPA’s proposal. Spectator also obtained three versions of the proposal, all of which were considered confidential.

An ‘intersection’ of interests: Expanding regional studies

The major’s focus on the Middle East developed over time.

When SIPA was part of the FAS, it managed all of the University’s regional studies institutes, according to Anderson, a scholar of the Middle East. However, after SIPA separated from the FAS in 2010, she said that the institutes, now without SIPA’s oversight, lost the “same kind of breadth of offerings they once had.”

So when the provost began reviewing regional studies in 2025, Anderson saw it as an opportunity for SIPA to return to its roots.

“There was a sort of intersection of SIPA’s interest and the provost’s interest in housing incremental offerings in regional studies, generally, and in Middle East, particularly,” Anderson said.

One version of SIPA’s proposal from January, obtained by Spectator, included the contemporary Middle East as a “focus area,” a set of courses students would have the option of selecting to specialize in a policy field. SIPA anticipated offering the set of courses by the second year of the major’s implementation. The proposal did not list the courses the focus area would include.

The school wrote in its January proposal that the Middle East focus would be “Phase I” of a larger plan to introduce focus areas for several other regions, including Africa, East Asia, Europe and Eurasia, Latin America, and South Asia.

However, the proposal states that SIPA would offer the other regional studies focus areas a year later and only “pending funding for relevant faculty hires.”

On the other hand, SIPA was already searching for three faculty members to teach courses on the Middle East and Israel, according to the provost’s review committee. Two of those professors would be jointly appointed with the Institute for Israel and Jewish Studies, in line with the University’s settlement with the federal government.

A University official denied that Columbia was prioritizing or singling out the Middle East, saying that the order in which the University would add regional studies offerings depended on COI approval and faculty availability.

SIPA later revised its proposal, replacing focus areas with “elective clusters,” pools of courses centering on the same topic, in an effort to offer students “greater flexibility,” according to SIPA’s April 1 proposal.

All classes in regional studies will be optional for students in the major, according to a University official.

The school proposed six elective clusters, with Middle East policy and politics as the only regional studies offering. Of the five courses listed as part of that cluster, four explicitly mention Israel or the Jewish world in their titles, though SIPA wrote that it might include three additional courses, which did not explicitly mention Israel, “over time.”

The school added in its April proposal that it “aims to expand the major” in the second and third year by introducing “expanded regional studies offerings.”

When asked why the major’s coursework prioritized Israel, Anderson said that since MESAAS did not focus on Israel and the IIJS did not take up contemporary policy questions, SIPA’s offerings could “complement what’s already here.”

Some faculty members have seen the decision in more political terms.

“The SIPA people have a breadth of expertise and points of view you will not find and are not likely to find in MESAAS—principally pro-Israel points of view, or things that are not lockstep postcolonial, anti-Zionist, and nothing else,” James Applegate, former chair of the senate education committee, said. “Having SIPA involved in having this major as an option for our students I think made a Columbia education much better, and not having it limits it and impoverishes it.”

Ester Fuchs, a SIPA professor and co-chair of Columbia’s Task Force on Antisemitism, said the task force had been advocating for measures, like new chairs in Middle East scholarship, to address “a big hole in the curriculum around Middle East politics and economics” long before Columbia reached its deal with the Trump administration, and it did not ask for them to be connected to the IIJS.

In its final December 2025 report, the task force recommended hiring tenure-track faculty experts on Middle East history, politics, political economy, and policy who were “not explicitly anti-Zionist.”

“The fact that this got connected in some way to the agreement, the University chose to do that. We did not ask,” Fuchs said. “These chairs originated through the task force, and we did not ask for that, and we were not consulted.”

In an April 1 report detailing Columbia’s compliance with its deal with the Trump administration, the University wrote that its searches for IIJS joint appointments were underway and that it had appointed long-term visiting faculty specializing in the Middle East to SIPA and the history and economics departments. These appointments were “already making significant curricular impact,” the University wrote in the report.

‘Quickly and secretly’: Backlash from faculty

Beyond the recommendation from the provost’s review of regional studies, the University has never publicly acknowledged any direct connection between the SIPA major and efforts to provide alternatives to MESAAS.

“Things are being done quickly and secretly, and I think that’s disastrous for the University,” Anya Schiffrin, Journalism ’00, senior lecturer at SIPA, said. “Secretly and quickly backfires because then people are suspicious, and they don’t get behind whatever the initiative is.”

Within the FAS, faculty members resented the time pressure the central administration applied to advance the major, as well as what they saw as a pernicious narrative that SIPA was innovative in a way that the FAS was not.

The central administration considered the program an “expressly stated university priority,” according to the COI’s December 2025 memo.

The policy and planning committee, the body elected to represent the FAS to the administration, had even floated a rule requiring proponents of new majors from outside the FAS to secure at least one cosponsor FAS department.

SIPA wrote, in a version of its proposal from the fall semester obtained by Spectator, that the “original design of the Major was intended to insure that no A&S Departments were expected to contribute to instruction,” except for a requirement for students to take Principles of Economics.

However, several SIPA faculty members hold joint appointments with FAS departments, and the April proposal states that certain non-SIPA electives could still count toward the major.

In light of the concerns from the FAS, the University convened a working group to examine the relationship between the liberal arts and professional schools this spring, and the COI issued a moratorium on new program proposals from the professional schools for the next year or until the group finished its work.

“We want to get things done,” Anderson said. “So, by the standards of a university, sometimes that may seem sort of aggressive.”

“I do think there was a measure of tension that ended up being sort of pushed upstairs,” she added. “The fault for that was neither SIPA nor Arts and Sciences, but ‘them,’ and I will leave it to Low Library to figure out how they reconcile that.”

After facing mounting backlash from the FAS, SIPA undertook a process of “substantive consultation” with several FAS departments and institutes in the spring semester, including MESAAS and the IIJS, according to its April proposal. It wrote that these consultations “provided very helpful feedback.”

“We didn’t have our finger on the pulse of some of the kinds of conversations going on within the Arts and Sciences,” Anderson said. “This is a way to come back to the Arts and Sciences and have those kinds of collaborations and conversations.”

SIPA wrote that it would continue to consult with FAS departments—a condition the COI attached to its approval—and it agreed to establish a standing coordination committee with the political science department. It wrote that it was “keen to replicate” this model with other departments and was working to do so with economics.

The COI wrote that these consultations would be “especially important” to prevent SIPA from creating courses which “overlap substantially” with those within the FAS.

Yet at its May 12 meeting, when the COI voted to approve the program, several committee members abstained. A person familiar with the deliberations said that the abstentions constituted an unusual act of opposition, given that the COI almost always conducts its work with unanimous approval.

“This was a new idea. It was an innovative idea. It could be very useful to the University, to the undergraduates,” Firestein, a member of both the COI and senate education committee who is also slated to teach in the major, said. “It could have problems that will have to be worked out over a couple of years, but should we take innovative programs and just purposely slow them down? I’m not for that personally, and I know that would be the criticism of COI.”

Suspicion also mounted over SIPA’s efforts to fundraise for the major.

Columbia has yet to release any information about the private donors for the program, but the COI indicated in its December 2025 memo that conversations with interested donors were ongoing.

“Refusal to disclose the donors lends credence to the view that there’s an agenda behind the new program,” Joseph Stiglitz, a University professor and Nobel laureate, wrote in a statement to Spectator.

A University official told Spectator that prospective donors were not involved in the development or design of the major and denied that the major’s approval process was secretive. Additional faculty hiring is still in progress and the University will announce more details as soon as they are confirmed, the official said, adding that SIPA publicized the program’s courses as soon as the COI approved them.

“We took a full year to do it. The other side of this is, if we had taken two years, people would say, ‘Gee, these committees just don’t do anything,’” Firestein said. “So which way do you want it?”

‘Reputational damage to the institution’: Backlash from the senate

The COI had a year to review the proposal. The University Senate, Columbia’s largest democratically elected body, had less than three months.

When the senate education committee first received SIPA’s proposal on Feb. 20, Columbia had already submitted it to the New York State Education Department, which officially registered the program in March.

The idea that the senate should even look at the proposal proved controversial. The University has long held that undergraduate programs do not require senate approval, a policy which Applegate, who served on the senate for more than 25 years, said was “part of the compromises that had created the senate.”

A University official told Spectator that Columbia sent the proposal to the senate education committee when the senate requested it.

Just four days after it received SIPA’s proposal, the education committee claimed in a document on its website that it had “statutory responsibility and duty” to review the program, saying that it “fundamentally changes the structure of undergraduate education within our institution.”

“We simply didn’t have precedent for something like this,” Ulrich Hengst, a faculty senator and co-chair of the education committee, said.

The SIPA major breaks from undergraduate majors offered by the professional schools in the past because it is primarily taught by the faculty of a graduate school outside the FAS. The computer science department falls under the School of Engineering and Applied Science, which has served undergraduates since 1864. The sustainable development major, offered through the Climate School, existed before Columbia founded the school in 2020 and is primarily taught through the FAS. The School of the Arts also offers undergraduate programs, but the school itself is within the FAS.

Still, some faculty members have vehemently opposed senate review of the program. Firestein said that the senate came “very close to infringing on academic freedom” by seeking to interfere with the COI, a faculty-led committee.

“Self-interest always comes masqueraded as high moral principle,” Applegate said. “There’s people who very, very much want the senate to review this, and so they’ll come up with a very creatively interpretive reading of history, statutes, et cetera that says we have to do this, and the answer is we don’t.”

But the full senate never reviewed it.

Rather, Hengst said the education committee sent its feedback to the COI and decided not to bring the program to a senate plenary.

It took student senators, who wrote a statement questioning whether the federal government was involved in the creation of the SIPA major, to spur a discussion of the major at the June 18 plenary.

“We have been repeatedly told that the major is not our right to question, that the major has been fully approved via normal procedures for undergraduate programs of study, and that our assertion that the major has any relationship to the federal government agreement is ‘reckless’ and ‘dangerous,’” the students wrote.

The University tried to stop the senators from issuing the statement in the first place, arguing that the statement could cause “reputational damage to the institution,” according to former student senator Helen Han Wei Luo, then-vice chair of the senate’s student affairs committee.

A University official told Spectator the administration raised concerns with the senate executive committee, which sets the agenda for plenaries, over the statement because of what they called its unsubstantiated claims.

Shipman responded to the student senators in a statement read at the June 18 plenary, denying the existence of “separate or undisclosed agreements with the government” concerning the creation of the SIPA major.

Firestein said that he considered suspicions that the major was connected to the settlement with the federal government “a conspiracy theory.”

Even with its motivations hotly contested, Columbia has moved ahead with the major.

“If you are a senator who believes that this program should be reviewed,” Hengst said, “then we are too late.”
